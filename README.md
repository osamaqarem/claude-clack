# claude-clack

[How to use this?](HOW_TO_USE.md)

> [!WARNING]
> This is pure slopware, generated on a whim and forgotten just as quickly.
>
> The only human creativity here was recording my keyboard clacking.

---

There is a particular kind of silence that settles over a desk when the typing
stops but the work does not. You hand a task to Claude Code, and for a few
seconds the machine thinks on your behalf. Files change. Logic rearranges
itself. Whole functions are born, edited, and retired — and through all of it,
nothing happens to your ears. The work is real, but it is mute. `claude-clack`
exists to fill that silence, and in filling it, to say something small but
genuine about how we relate to the tools that increasingly do our work for us.

### The disappearance of feedback

For most of the history of writing, the act left a trace you could feel. The
scratch of a nib, the thunk of a typebar striking a platen, the spring-loaded
resistance of a mechanical keyboard — each of these was a tiny contract between
intention and result. You pressed; the world pressed back. That loop of
proprioceptive feedback is older than computers, older than language written
down; it is how a body knows it has acted on the world.

Software has spent decades quietly dismantling that loop in the name of
efficiency. Touchscreens removed the travel. Autocomplete removed the
keystrokes. And now agentic coding tools remove the typing altogether: you
describe an outcome, and the edits arrive fully formed. This is, by almost any
measure, progress. But progress of this kind has a cost that rarely shows up on
a balance sheet — the erosion of the felt sense that something is happening.
When the agent edits ten files in a second, the most consequential moment of
your afternoon can pass without a single signal that your senses are equipped
to register.

### Why a sound, and why this sound

`claude-clack` answers that erosion with the cheapest, oldest trick available:
it makes a noise. Every time Claude runs `Edit`, `Write`, `MultiEdit`, or
`NotebookEdit`, a short burst of recorded keyboard clicks plays. Crucially, it
is a _burst_ and not a single beep. A lone click would read as a notification —
the language of alerts, of things that want your attention and then your
dismissal. A burst of jittered keystrokes reads as something else entirely: as
_work_. It is the sound of someone typing, because that is exactly what is
happening, even though the someone is not you and has no hands.

The details matter here in a way that is almost stubborn. The gaps between
clacks are randomized so the rhythm never falls into the tell-tale regularity of
a machine. Six subtly different samples are drawn from at random so the burst
never sounds looped. The count varies from edit to edit. All of this effort goes
toward a single illusion: that the sound is organic, that it has the small
imperfections of a living typist. The project could have shipped one click and a
fixed interval. It refuses to, because the point was never merely to make noise
— it was to make the _right_ noise, the one your nervous system already knows
how to interpret without being told.

### What it is really for

It would be easy to file `claude-clack` under novelty, and on some honest level
it belongs there; nobody needs their editor to clack. But the better way to read
it is as a small argument about attention. As more of our work is delegated to
agents that operate faster than we can watch, we will need new ways to stay in
the loop — not to micromanage every edit, but to feel the texture of work as it
happens, to know in our bodies that the machine is busy on our behalf. A glance
at a progress bar engages the eyes, which are usually already occupied. A sound
engages a channel that is almost always free, and it does so without demanding a
response.

So the clack is doing something quietly serious underneath the joke. It restores
a fragment of the ancient contract between pressing and being pressed back. It
turns invisible labor audible again. And it does this with a couple hundred lines
of shell, no dependencies you didn't already have, and a stubborn
insistence on getting the small details right. That is a good description of
most software worth keeping: humble in its means, clear about its single
purpose, and generous enough to make the work feel like work again.
